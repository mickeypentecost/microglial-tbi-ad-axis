# INJURY MEETS INHERITANCE — script & storyboard
*A shared microglial resolution-failure axis linking brain injury to Alzheimer's*
Est. runtime ~2:50 · 1920×1080 · 30 fps · Mickey Pentecost, PhD

Narration below is the current draft (v2). Scene durations in `src/theme.ts` are
fit to a ~145 wpm read of it.

## [0:00 — HOOK]
*Head impact and a DNA helix, converging on a central hub.*
A bad concussion can raise your risk of Alzheimer's decades later. So can the genes you inherit. Two roads, one destination. No one could explain how a physical hit and inherited DNA arrive at the same disease. I wanted to know if they run through the same wiring, inside one type of brain cell.

## [0:19 — THE CELL AND WHAT I BUILT]
*Zoom into a microglial cell; the accelerator and brake gene programs write out.*
That cell is the microglia, the brain's resident immune cell, and it's where Alzheimer's genetic risk concentrates. Working only from public data, I defined two opposing gene programs in advance — before looking at the results. An accelerator, the pro-inflammatory program. And a brake, the pro-resolving program built on TSG-6 and hyaluronan. Then I tested whether injury and inherited risk both hit the same one.

## [0:46 — THE DATA]
*Four data-type cards snap in, human and mouse; a "9 analyses" badge.*
I checked it four ways, in human and mouse. Single-nucleus RNA from microglia in Alzheimer's brains and injured mouse brains. Bulk RNA from sorted astrocytes. Spatial maps that keep each cell in place, around plaques and around the injury lesion. And brain proteomics. Nine analyses in all.

## [1:06 — CONVERGENCE]
*Accelerator gauge floored / brake flat + forest & scatter; then the field ignites at plaque and lesion beside the localization gradients.*
The accelerator converged in both — every way I looked. In brain injury and in Alzheimer's, the microglial accelerator is floored while the brake fails to engage. And it concentrates exactly where the damage is — at the lesion and beside the plaques.

## [1:31 — THE GENETICS]
*Genome shrinks to a 1.5% sliver → 31% / 21×; variants load the accelerator; threshold (APOE/TREM2) vs trigger (SPP1).*
Then the genetics gave direction. Thirty-one percent of inherited Alzheimer's risk sits in one and a half percent of the genome — a twenty-one-fold enrichment — and it loads onto the accelerator while avoiding the brake. Here's the sharp part. When I tested whether those variants control the inflammatory effector genes, they didn't. Inherited risk enters upstream, at APOE and TREM2. Downstream genes like SPP1 are installed by the injury or the disease itself. Threshold and trigger are separable. The two-hit model, made molecular.

## [2:07 — THE CD44 HUB]
*One receptor, two competing ligands; astrocytes ring a plaque; CD44 up across four modalities, TSG-6 down.*
Both arms are read out at one receptor: CD44. And it isn't only microglia — astrocytes ring the plaques and feed the same receptor. CD44 rises across four different measurements and both species, while the brake ligand TSG-6 falls. One receptor decides the outcome.

## [2:27 — WHY IT MATTERS]
*Three ranked interventions — reject "block the receptor", accept "block the ligand" and "provide TSG-6".*
This is where the statistic becomes a target. CD44 carries both signals — so blocking the receptor fails; you'd silence the brake along with the accelerator. Instead, rebalance the hub two ways: block the inflammatory ligand to remove the push, or provide TSG-6 in trans to restore the missing brake. Each is a ranked, testable prediction — a specific experiment, not a wish.

## [2:41 — CLOSE]
*Pull back to a whole, calm brain; the closing line; a "Built with Claude" end card.*
For decades we treated neurodegeneration like a runaway train. Maybe the answer isn't stopping the engine. It's giving the brain back its brakes.
